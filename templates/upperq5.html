Have you began writing your essays for college applications?





Great job! If you feel comfortable with your essays, it might be a good idea to have someone else read them and give you feedback. You are not obligated to rewrite your essay based on their critiques, but you will gain a better understanding of how other people may percieve your essays.

Your essays play a pretty big part in your college application. Done well, they can help make up for grades or test scores that are below the college’s average. Not to worry if you didn’t do so well in freshman English; here’s some tips to get you started:

How many essays?

Generally, if you’re applying to private colleges, many will use the Common Application. This means that you will likely have to write one essay with a 650-word limit on one of these prompts. In addition, each school may have a few supplementary essays, so make sure you know if they do. If you’re applying to the UC system, you’ll have to write four responses (each of which has a 350-word limit) to four of the eight prompts of your choosing.

Start Early

Ideally, start in the summer before the application season. You’ll have much more time to craft and revise your essays.

Your essay should tell a story

It should reveal something about you. If Admissions Officers can understand where you’re coming from, you’ll have a better shot of getting in. Click here to learn more about telling a story with your essay.

Your essays are not the time to plagiarize/practice style imitation

It wouldn’t be uncommon for you to read a really great essay and want to mimic it with your own. However, these essays are a way for you to share your personal experiences, and if your personal experiences haven’t led you to write a certain way up until now, now is not the best time to attempt someone else’s writing style. In other words, be genuine. Your essays will sound much better.
